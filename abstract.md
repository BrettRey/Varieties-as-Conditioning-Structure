# Varieties as Conditioning Structure

**Brett Reynolds**
Humber Polytechnic & University of Toronto

## Abstract

What distinguishes register from dialect from discourse community? Not what they are, but what they're conditioned on. This paper argues that these aren't three kinds of thing but three dimensions of participant expectation, each conditioned on a different anchor: SITUATION (the here-and-now), ASCRIPTION (how the speaker is classified), or IDENTIFICATION (whose norms the speaker orients to). Existing frameworks tend to conflate ascription (what you are treated as) with identification (who you orient to). Separating them reveals that their divergence is a major locus of sociolinguistic agency, and the framework predicts that sociolinguistic variables should cluster at the points where S, A, and I make divergent predictions. Drawing on Wiese's (2023) account of communicative situations and O'Connor's (2019) evolutionary game theory, I distinguish exogenous stabilizers (S/A/I themselves) from an endogenous stabilizer (coordination costs), and argue that varieties are homeostatic property cluster categories: conventional enough to be coordination equilibria, structured enough to support reliable inferences. A worked example using Kiesling's (1998) fraternity data on (ING) demonstrates that the framework captures distributional structure that standard factor-group analyses conflate.

**Keywords:** sociolinguistic variation, register, dialect, discourse community, homeostatic property clusters, coordination games, Bayesian conditioning, participant expectation, indexicality, ascription, identification
